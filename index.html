Research

My research focuses on the design of application-specific integrated circuits (ASICs) that accelerate computationally hard problems — particularly combinatorial optimization and deep learning inference. I draw on physics, computer architecture, and algorithm design to build systems that are orders of magnitude faster and more efficient than general-purpose processors.

Circuit Design
Oscillator-Based Ising & Potts Machines
Ising machines are physical systems that naturally evolve toward low-energy states, making them powerful solvers for NP-hard combinatorial optimization problems such as Max-Cut and graph coloring. I design and build CMOS ring oscillator networks that emulate this behavior in silicon. Coupled oscillators synchronize in patterns that encode the solution to an optimization problem, achieving results that classical digital processors cannot match in energy efficiency. My work covers both the circuit-level design of coupled CMOS oscillators and the ASIC microarchitecture that orchestrates them at scale.
Architecture
ASIC Accelerators for Combinatorial Optimization
I architect many-core ASIC accelerators that stream large graph workloads onto arrays of pipelined solver cores. Key challenges include tiling and partitioning graphs too large to fit on-chip, designing efficient on-chip interconnects and NoC fabrics, and balancing compute density with memory bandwidth. My accelerators achieve significant speedups over GPU implementations while consuming a fraction of the power — enabling real-world applications in logistics, drug discovery, and hardware design verification.
Low Power
Deep Learning on Adiabatic Logic
Conventional CMOS logic dissipates energy every time a transistor switches. Adiabatic (charge recovery) logic recycles this switching energy, offering a fundamentally different energy scaling law. I design deep learning inference engines — including pipelined matrix multiplication and vector processing units for transformer models — using adiabatic logic families, exploring the architectural trade-offs of building neural network accelerators that consume dramatically less power.
GPU / TPU
GPU & TPU Algorithm Optimization
Before committing an algorithm to silicon, I optimize it extensively on programmable accelerators. For GPUs, I write custom CUDA kernels applying kernel fusion, quantization, and graph reordering to exploit the memory hierarchy. For Google TPUs, I reformulate solver algorithms in JAX to map cleanly onto systolic array architectures. This cross-platform work produces practical high-performance solvers and deep insight into which algorithmic patterns are worth moving to custom hardware.
